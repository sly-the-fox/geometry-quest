12. CRITICAL DESIGN PRINCIPLES
Principle 1 — Geometry Is Functional, Not Decorative Every geometric element must have a mechanical purpose. The visual geometry teaches mathematical relationships. Players learn through doing, not through reading.
Principle 2 — Healing Over Violence Bosses cannot be defeated through damage alone. Understanding and compassion are mechanically stronger than force. Violence increases corruption values — healing decreases them.
Principle 3 — The 4-Sided Pyramid Is Always the Problem Whenever the player sees square-based geometry, it signals corruption, entrapment, or duality. The player's tools always move things toward 3-sided (tetrahedron) fluidity and beyond. This is the single most important visual language rule in the game.
Principle 4 — Process Order Matters The real-world energy work has a specific sequence: observe first (7-sided), disrupt if needed (5 or 11-sided), stabilize afterward (6-sided), complete and integrate (8 and 12-sided). Puzzles and boss fights that violate this order should always fail or become harder. The game teaches this order through consequence.
Principle 5 — The 11-Sided Is Never Safe Alone The Hendecagon Disruptor is the most powerful breaking tool but always carries self-damage and chaos risk. Any game system using it must enforce the stabilization follow-up window. This mechanic teaches the real principle: you cannot liberate a stuck pattern and leave the field open — you must stabilize what you break.
Principle 6 — Emergent Complexity From Simple Rules Three stats (coherence, resonance, stability, flow), 14 geometric tools, and 16 elemental types combine into a vast possibility space. Design each element to be simple alone and complex in combination.
Principle 7 — Community As Sacred Circle Multiplayer mechanics should reinforce that helping others strengthens everyone. The Tetrad formation (4 players) should be the most powerful structure in the game — more powerful than anything achievable solo — because it embodies the full elemental harmony.
